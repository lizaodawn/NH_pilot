all kinds of pears are indigestible food, even for men in health; and to the sick they are as strictly forbidden as wine. cooked, however, they are remarkably wholesome and pleasant, especially those of crustumium. all kinds of pears, however, if boiled down with honey are wholesome to the stomach. out of pears are made plasters for dispersing flesh lesions, and they use a decoction of them for iudurations. by themselves they neutralize the poison of toadstools and tree-fungi, expelling it by their weight in addition to the counteracting effect of their juice. the wild pear is very slow in ripening. sliced the pears are hung up and dried for checking looseness of the bowels, for which purpose a decoction also of them is efficacious, taken as drink. a decoction also of the leaves with the fruit is used for the same purposes. the ashes of pear wood are even more efficacious against the poison of tree-fungi. apples and pears, even a small quantity, make a remarkably heavy load for beasts of burden. it is said that a remedy for this is to give them a few to eat, or at least to show some, before beginning the journey.
