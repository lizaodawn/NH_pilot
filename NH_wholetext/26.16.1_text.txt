bechion is also called tussilago. there are two kinds of it. wherever the wild kind grows it is believed that springs run under the surface, and the plant is considered a sign by the water-finders. the leaves are rather larger than those of ivy, numbering five or seven, whitish underneath and pale on the upper side. there is no stem, or flower, or seed, and the root is slender. some think it is the same as areion, and chamaeleuce under another name. the smoke of this plant, dried with the root and burnt, is said to cure, if inhaled deeply through a reed, an inveterate cough, but the patient must take a sip of raisin wine at each inhalation.
